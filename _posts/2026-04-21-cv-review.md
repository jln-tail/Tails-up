## Introduction

I've studied and worked in computer vision for around two years now. There have been failures, but I have also learned a great deal. Some of that learning has been indirect: by discovering what I am not good at and identifying the parts of the field I still do not understand well. To take a thorough look at my current knowledge, and to start filling in some of the blank spaces, I wrote this article.

Suggestions are welcome through GitHub Issues in this blog repository.

## 0. Scope, assumptions, and how to read this article

### 0.1 The scope of this article

This article focuses on deep learning-based models in modern computer vision.

It does not cover computer vision methods from before the neural network era. In other words, image processing techniques based directly on pixel values and related operators are not discussed here, as they are treated as part of the foundational knowledge of computer vision.

Please note that some of the information here may be incomplete or outdated due to the limits of my own knowledge, so the date of writing should always be taken into account when reading this article.

Some parts of the language in this article were polished with AI assistance for clarity and readability. The content, structure, and technical views remain my own.

## 1. Tasks and models
Computer vision is often discussed along two different axes: tasks and models. This is because some models — or, more broadly, some paradigms — can be applied across multiple tasks. This cross-task nature makes it difficult to give a fully structured overview of the whole field in a single table or diagram.

Since this article focuses more on model families and implementation details, the later sections are organized by models rather than by tasks. Still, I think it is useful to briefly introduce the major task categories in computer vision, since they appear so frequently in both research and practical discussions. The following table summarizes some of the large task categories that are commonly referenced in the field.

## Major CV tasks

| Task | Typical Input | Typical Output | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Classification | Image | Class label | Predict a category for the whole image.  |
| Detection | Image | Bounding boxes + class labels | Localize and classify objects.|
| Segmentation | Image | Pixel-wise masks or labels | Predict structured regions at the pixel level. |
| Generation | Noise, prompt, image, or condition | Generated or edited image | Synthesize or modify visual content.  |
| 3D Reconstruction & View Synthesis | One or more images / views | 3D scene representation or novel view | Recover scene structure or render unseen views. |
| Vision-Language Understanding | Image + text | Text output, matching score, or aligned representation | Connect visual inputs with language. |

There are also several other common tasks that are frequently discussed in computer vision, even though they are not covered in detail in this article.

### Other common tasks often discussed in computer vision

| Task | Typical Input | Typical Output | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tracking | Video | Object trajectories across frames | Common in video understanding and surveillance |
| Pose / Keypoint Estimation | Image or video | Keypoints / skeletons | Often used for humans, hands, or faces |
| Depth Estimation | Image or stereo pair | Depth map | Closely related to 3D perception |
| Optical Flow / Motion Estimation | Two or more frames | Motion field | Important for video and geometric reasoning |
| Image Retrieval / Representation Learning | Image or image-text pair | Embedding or ranked matches | Important for search, matching, and pretraining |
| Video Understanding | Video | Labels, captions, events, or temporal localization | Extends image-based CV into the temporal domain |

It is also worth noting that, in industrial practice, real-world systems often combine multiple tasks rather than solving only one in isolation. Taking the popular example of autonomous driving, a production system may rely on real-time detection and 3D reconstruction to model its surroundings, while also using classification or vision-language methods to identify pedestrians, traffic signs, or other important scene elements. For this reason, being familiar with common task categories and their typical solutions is helpful for engineers when making technical choices and designing practical systems.
